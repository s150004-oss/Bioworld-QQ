Introduction to Fungi

Fungi consist of eukaryotic cells enclosed in a cell wall of chitin (unlike the cellulose cell walls of plants). They range in size and complexity from unicellular yeasts to elaborate cup fungi and mushrooms.

Examine morphology of yeast cells
Study filamentous fungi structures
Observe mushroom anatomy and spores
Investigate lichen symbiosis

Key Characteristics of Fungi

  • Cell Wall: Made of chitin (not cellulose like plants)
  • Nutrition: Heterotrophic (absorb nutrients from environment)
  • Body Structure: Composed of hyphae (filaments) that form mycelium
  • Reproduction: Via spores (sexual and asexual)
  • Ecological Role: Decomposers, pathogens, symbionts

1. Yeasts (Unicellular Fungi)

Objective

To examine the morphology of yeast cells, including their budding reproduction method.

Materials

  • Yeast culture
  • Iodine solution
  • Microscope slides and coverslips
  • Oil immersion objective

Procedure

1
Take one drop of yeast suspension
2
Add a drop of water-iodine solution (3:1 ratio)
3
Place a cover slip and examine under high power and oil immersion
4
Make drawings to show cell shape and arrangement

What to Look For

  • Cell Shape: Round or oval cells, larger than bacteria
  • Budding: Look for smaller cells attached to larger ones
  • Intracellular Granules: Stained by iodine - likely glycogen storage
  • Arrangement: Single cells, pairs, or small clusters

Questions to Answer

  1. What was the colour of the intracellular granules?
  2. What is the most probable chemical nature of these granules?
  3. What probable function do these granules serve for the cells?

2. Filamentous Fungi

Unlike yeasts, filamentous fungi have hyphae that are either septate (with cross-walls) or coenocytic (without cross-walls). The mycelium produces specialized reproductive hyphae for spore production.

Yeast

Unicellular, non-filamentous fungi that reproduce by budding. Used in baking and brewing.

Filamentous Fungi

Form hyphae and mycelium. Includes molds like Rhizopus and Penicillium.

Mushrooms

Fruiting bodies of certain fungi that produce basidiospores.

Lichens

Symbiotic associations between fungi and photosynthetic algae or cyanobacteria.

A. Rhizopus (Bread Mold)

1
Add a drop of Lactophenol cotton blue to a clean slide
2
Transfer mycelia from Rhizopus growing on bread using a sterilized needle
3
Spread the mycelia, place a cover slip, and examine under microscope (40X)
4
Draw hyphae, sporangium, sporangiophores, rhizoids, and spores

B. Penicillium (Blue Cheese Mold)

Penicillium is added to blue cheese to give it flavor. Examine a small piece of cheese containing Penicillium under the microscope.

C. Mushroom Anatomy

Part Description Function
Cap (Pileus) Upper part of the fruiting body Protects gills; aids in spore dispersal
Gills (Lamellae) Thin structures under the cap Produce and release basidiospores
Stipe Stem-like structure Supports the cap; may contain reproductive structures
Basidiospores Microscopic reproductive cells Dispersal and reproduction
Mycelium Underground network of hyphae Nutrient absorption; vegetative growth

3. Lichens - Symbiotic Associations

Lichens are symbiotic associations between a fungus (mycobiont) and a photosynthetic partner (photobiont) - either green algae or cyanobacteria.

Symbiotic Relationship

  • Fungal Partner: Provides structure, protection, and absorbs minerals/water
  • Algal Partner: Provides carbohydrates through photosynthesis
  • Mutual Benefits: Both organisms benefit from the association

Procedure for Lichen Observation

  1. Examine the provided slide of lichen cross-section
  2. Identify the fungal hyphae and algal cells
  3. Draw and label both partners in the association
  4. Note the arrangement - algae are often in a distinct layer

Questions About Lichens

  1. What is the function of the algal and fungal partners in the lichen symbiosis?
  2. How could lichens be used as indicators of environmental pollution?

Safety Notes

  • Lactophenol cotton blue is toxic - avoid skin contact and inhalation
  • Use proper technique when flaming needles for sterilization
  • Handle microscope slides carefully to avoid breakage
  • Dispose of fungal cultures properly after observation
