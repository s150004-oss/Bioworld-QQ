Introduction to Bacteria

Bacteria are prokaryotic cells lacking membrane-bound organelles. They are simple compared to eukaryotic cells but exhibit remarkable diversity in shape, size, and metabolism.

Examine bacterial morphology (shapes & arrangements)
Perform Gram staining technique
Use oil immersion microscopy (100X)

Special Considerations for Bacteria Observation

  • Size: Bacteria are small (0.5-5μm) - similar to wavelength of visible light
  • Color: Most are colorless with refractive index greater than water
  • Microscopy: Require oil immersion objectives for sufficient resolution
  • Staining: Need staining to visualize under light microscope

Experiment A: Bacterial Cell Shapes

Objective

To examine the morphology of bacterial cells based on their shape, size, and arrangement.

Materials

  • Prepared slides of: Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Bacillus, Spirilla
  • Microscope with oil immersion lens (100X)
  • Immersion oil

Cocci

Spherical bacteria. Arrangements: singles, diplococci, streptococci (chains), staphylococci (clusters).

Bacilli

Rod-shaped bacteria. Can be single or in chains (streptobacilli). Some are short (coccobacilli).

Spirilla

Spiral-shaped bacteria. Includes spirilla (rigid) and spirochetes (flexible).

Bacterial Shape Examples Arrangements Size Range
Cocci (Spherical) Staphylococcus, Streptococcus Singles, pairs, chains, clusters, tetrads 0.5-1.0 μm diameter
Bacilli (Rod-shaped) Bacillus, Escherichia coli Singles, pairs, chains 0.5-1.0 × 1-10 μm
Spirilla (Spiral) Spirillum, Treponema Usually single cells 0.1-1.0 × 5-250 μm
Vibrios (Comma-shaped) Vibrio cholerae Singles or pairs 0.5 × 1.5-3.0 μm

Oil Immersion Technique

  1. First focus under 40X objective
  2. Half rotate the objective so no lens is in position
  3. Put a drop of immersion oil on the slide
  4. Complete rotation to 100X objective position
  5. Refocus using FINE FOCUS ONLY (never use coarse focus with 100X)
  6. Clean oil from lens with lens paper after use

Experiment B: Gram Staining of Bacteria

Objective

To perform Gram staining technique and differentiate bacteria into Gram-positive and Gram-negative groups.

Gram-Positive

Thick peptidoglycan layer
Retains crystal violet stain
Appears purple/violet

Gram-Negative

Thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane
Loses crystal violet, takes up safranin
Appears red/pink

Gram Staining Procedure

1
Heat fix: Make thin bacterial smear, air dry, pass through flame 3 times
2
Crystal violet: Stain for 1 minute, wash with water
3
Gram's iodine: Flood with iodine for 1 minute (forms CV-I complex)
4
Decolorize: 95% alcohol for 30-60 seconds (CRITICAL STEP)
5
Counterstain: Safranin for 1 minute, wash, air dry
6
Observe: Under oil immersion (100X)

Safety Precautions

  • Disinfect working area with alcohol before and after
  • Incinerate inoculation loop before and after use
  • Wash hands with disinfectant after handling bacteria
  • Keep petri dishes closed when not in use
  • Dispose of materials properly

Gram Stain Interpretation

  • Gram-positive: Dark violet/purple (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus subtilis)
  • Gram-negative: Red/pink (e.g., Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas, Rhizobium)
  • Gram-variable: Some cells stain differently in same culture (common in older cultures)

Experiment C: Bacteria from Yogurt

Objective

To stain and identify Lactobacillus and Streptococcus bacteria in yogurt.

Background

Yogurt is produced by fermentation of milk by lactic acid bacteria: Lactobacillus bulgaricus and Streptococcus thermophilus. These convert lactose to lactic acid, which thickens milk and inhibits spoilage organisms.
