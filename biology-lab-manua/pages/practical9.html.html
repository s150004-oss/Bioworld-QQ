Practical 9: Membrane Diffusion and Transport

Investigating passive and active transport across selectively permeable membranes, osmosis, and plasmolysis in plant cells

Key Concepts

Diffusion: Passive movement of molecules from high to low concentration. Osmosis: Diffusion of water across a semipermeable membrane. Selective Permeability: Membranes allow some molecules to pass while restricting others.

Membrane Selectivity Demo

Selectively Permeable Membrane: Small water molecules pass through pores, while larger sugar molecules and ions cannot.

Hypotonic

Lower solute concentration outside cell. Water moves INTO cell. Plant cells become turgid.

Isotonic

Equal solute concentration inside and outside. No net water movement. Dynamic equilibrium.

Hypertonic

Higher solute concentration outside cell. Water moves OUT of cell. Plant cells plasmolyze.

Osmometer Principle

Water moves through the semipermeable membrane into the sucrose solution, increasing the liquid height in the tube. The greater the sucrose concentration, the higher the osmotic pressure, and the more water enters the tube.

Plasmolysis Process

When plant cells are placed in hypertonic solutions, water leaves the cell by osmosis. The cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall, creating gaps. This is plasmolysis. The cell wall remains intact, but the protoplast (living part) contracts.

Procedure:

  1. Prepare wet mount of Rhoeo discolor or onion epidermis in distilled water (hypotonic).
  2. Observe turgid cells with cytoplasm pressed against cell walls.
  3. Replace with 0.8M sucrose solution (hypertonic) and wait 5 minutes.
  4. Observe plasmolysis - cytoplasm pulling away from cell walls.
  5. Calculate 50% plasmolysis point using different sucrose concentrations.

Selective Permeability Results

Small molecules like NaOH and iodine can pass through dialysis tubing pores, while large starch molecules cannot. This demonstrates molecular size-based selectivity of membranes.

Key Findings:

  • The 50% plasmolysis point indicates the internal solute concentration of the plant cells
  • Water always moves from areas of high water potential to low water potential
  • Animal cells lyse in hypotonic solutions (no cell wall), while plant cells become turgid
  • Plasmolysis is reversible if cells are returned to hypotonic solution quickly
