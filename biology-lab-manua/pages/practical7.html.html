Practical 7: Identification of Biological Molecules in Foods

Applying biochemical tests to detect reducing sugars, starch, lipids, and proteins in various food samples

Objective

To apply knowledge of biochemical tests to identify specific classes of biological molecules present in common foods and understand their nutritional composition.

Tests Overview

Procedure Summary

E
Biochemical Reactions in Foods

Key Steps:

  1. Preparation: Create extracts from food samples (potato, onion, dates).
  2. Reducing Sugars Test: Add Benedict's reagent, heat in water bath, observe color change.
  3. Starch Test: Add iodine solution, observe immediate color change.
  4. Lipids Test: Apply samples to filter paper, add Sudan III dye, rinse, observe stains.
  5. Proteins Test: Add Biuret reagent to samples, observe color change.
  6. Analysis: Compare results with control (water) to determine presence/absence of molecules.

Scientific Principles

Reducing Sugars

Sugars with free aldehyde or ketone groups can reduce Cu²⁺ to Cu⁺ oxide, causing color change in Benedict's reagent.

Starch-Iodine Complex

Iodine fits into the helical structure of amylose in starch, forming a blue-black charge-transfer complex.

Hydrophobic Interaction

Sudan III dye is non-polar and dissolves in lipid regions, staining them orange due to hydrophobic interactions.

Biuret Reaction

Peptide bonds in proteins form violet complexes with copper ions in alkaline conditions (coordination complex).

Key Takeaways

  • Different foods contain different types and amounts of biological molecules.
  • Dates are rich in reducing sugars, potatoes in starch, olive oil in lipids, and egg white in proteins.
  • Biochemical tests provide qualitative analysis of food composition.
  • Control experiments (water) are essential to validate test results.
  • Understanding food composition helps in nutritional analysis and diet planning.
