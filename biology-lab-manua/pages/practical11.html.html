Practical 11: Purification of the Enzyme Amylase

Extracting, testing, and analyzing the enzyme amylase from barley seeds, investigating its catalytic activity and temperature sensitivity

What is Amylase?

Amylase is a digestive enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis of starch into smaller carbohydrates (maltose, glucose). It's produced in seeds to break down starch reserves during germination, providing energy for plant growth.

Objectives

Extract Amylase

Isolate the enzyme amylase from germinated barley seeds using grinding, centrifugation, and methanol precipitation.

Test Enzyme Activity

Verify amylase activity by testing its ability to convert starch into reducing sugars (glucose).

Investigate Temperature Effects

Examine how temperature affects amylase activity, including enzyme denaturation at high temperatures.

Enzyme-Substrate Interaction

AMYLASE

Substrate: Starch

Complex carbohydrate (polysaccharide) made of glucose units. Tested with iodine (blue-black color).

Enzyme: Amylase

Protein catalyst that breaks glycosidic bonds in starch. Optimal activity at 37°C.

Products: Glucose/Maltose

Simple sugars (mono/disaccharides). Tested with Benedict's reagent (green→red color change).

Step 1: Seed Germination

Germinate barley seeds for 4 days to activate amylase production for starch breakdown.

Amylase activity increases during germination
Step 2: Grinding & Extraction

Grind germinated seeds in peptone solution to break cells and release cellular contents.

Peptone prevents enzyme degradation
Step 3: Centrifugation

Centrifuge at 2500 rpm for 5 min to remove cell debris, organelles, and membranes.

Collect supernatant containing amylase
Step 4: Methanol Precipitation

Add methanol to supernatant; amylase precipitates while other molecules remain soluble.

Amylase is insoluble in alcohol
Step 5: Resuspension

Resuspend precipitated amylase in distilled water for activity testing.

Creates enzyme preparation for testing
Step 6: Activity Tests

Test amylase preparation with starch and Benedict's reagent to confirm enzyme activity.

Starch + Amylase → Glucose (Benedict's positive)

Temperature Effects on Enzyme Activity

E
0°C (Low activity)
37°C (Optimal)
60°C (Denaturing)
100°C (Denatured)

Low Temperature (0-10°C)

Low kinetic energy = slow reaction rate. Enzyme intact but inactive.

Optimal Temperature (37°C)

Maximum reaction rate. Enzyme structure stable and active.

High Temperature (60°C+)

Denaturation begins. Hydrogen bonds break = loss of 3D structure.

Boiling (100°C)

Complete denaturation. Irreversible loss of enzyme activity.

Safety Precautions

  • Methanol is poisonous - do not inhale or contact skin
  • Balance centrifuge tubes properly before operation
  • Always label tubes - never rely on memory
  • Use Benedict's reagent with care (contains copper sulfate)
  • Wear lab coat, gloves, and safety goggles

Key Concepts & Questions

Why use methanol for precipitation?

Amylase (a protein) is insoluble in alcohol. Methanol disrupts hydration shells around proteins, causing them to aggregate and precipitate out of solution.

What happens during denaturation?

High temperature breaks hydrogen bonds and other non-covalent interactions, causing the protein to lose its 3D structure. The active site is disrupted, and enzyme activity is lost.

Thermophilic enzymes

Some organisms (thermophiles) have enzymes stable at high temperatures (up to 100°C). Their proteins have more ionic bonds and hydrophobic interactions for heat stability.

Control experiments importance

Testing enzyme preparation for starch and glucose before activity tests ensures no contamination. This validates that any glucose produced comes from amylase action on starch.
