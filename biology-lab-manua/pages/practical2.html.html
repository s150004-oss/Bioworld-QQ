Practical Objectives

Protista are single eukaryotic cell organisms with no cell wall. Compared with a human cell, they are immensely complex, especially in the most advanced group, like the ciliates. A protist is a whole organism in a single cell.

Observe aspects of behavior and diversity in Protist videos
Study and draw characteristics of different types of Protists
Observe behavior of live Protista in pond water

Materials Needed

  • Plastic pipettes, microscope slides, cover slips
  • Permanent slides of Paramecium, Bursaria, Euglena, Amoeba, Arcella
  • Fresh pond water, "Protoslo" or "Detain" (to slow fast-moving protists)
  • Vinegar/dilute acetic acid

Video Summaries

Video 1: Flagellates & Amoebas (15 minutes)

Protists have eukaryotic cells, so they differ from bacteria in having a nucleus and cell organelles. They can be divided into 3 main groups depending on their locomotion:

Flagellates

Move using whip-like flagella. Examples: Euglena, Trypanosoma, Volvox

Amoebas

Move using pseudopodia (false feet). Examples: Amoeba, Arcella, Actinopods

Ciliates

Move using numerous hair-like cilia. Examples: Paramecium, Bursaria, Vorticella

Video 2: The Ciliates (15 minutes)

Decaying plants in a pond are attacked by a range of Ciliates. You will learn about their complex cellular structures including oral grooves, contractile vacuoles, and dual nuclei system.

Key Protist Structures

  • Contractile Vacuole: Removes excess water from the cell
  • Oral Groove: Channel leading to the cytostome (mouth)
  • Macronucleus: Controls daily cell activities
  • Micronucleus: Involved in sexual reproduction
  • Pellicle: Flexible "skin" that gives shape to ciliates

Observing Protists

Tips for Finding and Focusing Protists

  • Protists are small and many are transparent - search systematically under high power (40X)
  • Use a search pattern to cover the entire slide methodically
  • Focus can be challenging - don't focus on dust on the cover slip!
  • First focus on part of the slide with no cover slip to establish reference point
  • Adjust iris diaphragm for maximum sharpness (but not too far or light will be insufficient)

Live Protist Observation

Procedure for Observing Live Protists in Pond Water

  1. Collect pond water in a jar and add decomposing leaves
  2. Let it age for 5-6 days (provided in lab)
  3. Use a dropper to pipette a small drop of pond water on a clean slide
  4. Add a drop of "Detain" or "Protoslo" to slow fast-moving protists
  5. Place a coverslip and observe under low (10X) and high power (40X)
  6. Make a sketch of organisms found
  7. Describe observed behaviors: movement, feeding, obstacle avoidance

Behavioral Observations

When observing live protists, note:

  • Movement patterns: Do they swim in straight lines, spiral, or meander?
  • Feeding behavior: Can you observe phagocytosis or filter feeding?
  • Response to stimuli: Do they move toward or away from light?
  • Interaction with environment: How do they navigate around obstacles?
